How can I create a portfolio that stands out and actively demonstrates my UX design skills through real-world application?

Understanding The Problem

The UX industry is highly competitive, particularly for junior designers who are just emerging from study and looking to break into the field. Without real client work to show, I needed a way to stand out.

To stand out in a competitive industry as a junior designer, I reimagined my portfolio as a live UX case study with interactive surveys for hiring managers teaching myself backend development along the way to build and host it independently.

When Did I Realise That This Could Be My Solution

I realised this early on while researching job opportunities—most roles preferred candidates with prior experience. That made it clear I needed to find a way to demonstrate value without relying on past clients. I started ideating around how to turn this limitation into a strength.

A portfolio is more than just a container for work it can be a live, usable product. By treating it as a project in itself, I could show what I’m capable of in real time. More importantly, I could bridge the gap between myself and my target market by making the hiring manager an active participant in the process.

The Approach

I began by asking a simple question: what should a UX portfolio actually do? To find out, I looked at a range of portfolios from successful designers. I paid attention to how they presented their work, how their content was structured, what felt effective, and what didn’t. Through this research, one thing became clear. If I’m applying for UX roles, my portfolio needs to reflect good UX. It should be easy to navigate, respectful of the viewer’s time, and engaging enough to stand out.

I realised that reaching my target audience, hiring managers, wasn’t going to be easy. Most portfolios don’t provide a way for the viewer to participate or leave feedback, which felt like a missed opportunity. I saw a gap.

That’s when I started thinking differently. What if my portfolio wasn’t a static showcase, but a living product? Something that could grow and evolve through real user input. That thought became the foundation for what you’re experiencing now. It is a live case study shaped by real feedback.

Wireframes

To explore different ways of structuring the case study layout, I experimented with two wireframe directions:

Initially, I planned to use a full-page scrolling layout, where each section took up the full screen. It looked polished, but forced a linear flow and reduced the user’s ability to scan or jump freely between sections. It felt like a designer-driven decision, not a user-centred one—so I scrapped it.

    I also intentionally cut:
  • Complex 3D animations
  • Heavy page transitions
  • Auto-scroll features

These were removed to keep the experience focused, performant, and distraction-free—especially for busy hiring managers.

Extended Content Mode (In Development)

The Extended Content toggle lets you control how much detail you see. ’Extended mode' reveals extra process insights with a smooth typing animation. Toggle it anytime, no reloads, no interruptions.

In my research, I found that a common issue with portfolios is either being overloaded with information or too minimal, lacking enough detail. To solve this, Im introducing a toggle that lets users choose the level of content they want to see. This keeps the experience immersive while also being respectful of the user's time.

Survey

The embedded survey system creates a continuous feedback loop, turning every interaction into real-time insight on what works and what’s unclear, and using that input to guide future iterations of the portfolio.

The feedback is submitted through a form connected to my Firebase backend. Each response is stored along with the:

  • Timestamp
  • Question answered
  • User's selected response

This setup allows me to review input in real time and understand how users are interacting with the portfolio. The data helps me identify what’s working and where improvements are needed, directly supporting the iterative goal of the project.
